Artificial Intelligence at CBU

California Baptist University is committed to the responsible, ethical, and secure use of artificial intelligence in support of teaching, learning, research, and institutional operations.

Microsoft Copilot (Enterprise / Edu) is the university‑approved generative AI platform.

Overview: Importance, Potential, and Awareness

California Baptist University believes each person has been created for a unique purpose. In that spirit, artificial intelligence (AI)—including generative AI—can equip CBU staff and faculty to serve students more effectively and to strengthen our Christ‑centered educational mission by supporting excellent academics alongside spiritual and social development.

To honor that purpose, AI must be employed with wisdom and care in ways that protect students, employees, and the university. This page outlines why responsible AI use matters, the opportunities it can create, and the shared awareness habits that guide faithful, responsible use of AI at CBU.

Why Responsible AI Use Matters

  • Trust and integrity: CBU’s teaching, research, and operations depend on accuracy, transparency, and accountability—AI outputs must be reviewed and verified before use.
  • Privacy and confidentiality: AI tools can store, process, or expose sensitive information if used improperly, including student records, HR data, contracts, and protected research or study data.
  • Compliance and risk management: Responsible AI use helps meet legal, regulatory, accreditation, and contractual obligations while reducing institutional and reputational risk.
  • Human dignity and impartiality: CBU seeks to treat students, applicants, and employees with biblical fairness and care. AI outputs should be reviewed and corrected to support just outcomes, equitable impacts, and impartial decision‑making.
  • Academic and professional standards: Clear expectations support academic integrity and appropriate attribution, authorship, and citation practices in both academic and professional work.

Potential Opportunities Enabled by AI

  • Student support and success: Drafting clearer instructions, improving feedback rubrics, creating practice questions, and generating examples—while keeping humans accountable for final decisions.
  • Teaching and learning design: Brainstorming activities, differentiating content, improving accessibility (such as plain‑language rewrites), and creating formative assessments.
  • Research productivity: Literature discovery support, outline generation, code assistance, and summarization—paired with discipline‑appropriate validation and citation.
  • Administrative efficiency: Summarizing meeting notes, drafting communications, creating first‑pass FAQs, accelerating routine writing tasks, routine reporting and analysis needs.
  • IT and analytics: Faster troubleshooting, documentation, and prototype development using reviewed and approved tools.

AI Use Case Highlights

Generative AI technologies offer practical solutions across university departments, enhancing efficiency, communication, and decision‑making. The examples below illustrate how approved generative AI tools can support campus operations when used with appropriate human oversight and in alignment with CBU policy.

Student Success Counselors

Student success counselors may use generative AI tools to draft personalized responses for students seeking assistance with academic programs, course selection, or scheduling questions.

By grounding the AI tool in approved program requirements, standard guidance, and common student inquiries, counselors can more efficiently generate tailored draft responses.

All AI‑assisted content is reviewed, edited, and approved by counselors prior to being shared with students to ensure accuracy, clarity, and a supportive, personal tone.

Safety Services Shift Supervisors

Safety Services shift supervisors may use generative AI tools to extract key information from dispatch call logs or operational notes and generate initial draft summaries to support shift transitions and reporting.

These drafts help supervisors prepare documentation more efficiently while maintaining consistency across reports.

Supervisors remain responsible for validating all information, correcting errors, and finalizing reports before distribution or archival.

Admissions Staff

Admissions staff may use generative AI tools to draft responses to common applicant inquiries, summarize application trends, or prepare first‑pass outreach communications for prospective students.

By using approved messaging frameworks, published admissions criteria, and publicly available information, AI tools can assist staff in creating timely and consistent draft communications.

Admissions personnel are responsible for reviewing all AI‑assisted drafts to ensure accuracy, policy compliance, and alignment with institutional messaging before communications are finalized or sent.

These examples demonstrate how generative AI can assist with routine drafting and summarization tasks while preserving human judgment, accountability, and institutional responsibility.

Awareness: What “Using AI the Right Way” Looks Like

  • Use approved tools for university work: When possible, use CBU‑approved AI tools and follow the IT review process for any new tools or pilot initiatives.
  • Protect sensitive data: Do not enter confidential, regulated, or personally identifiable information into AI tools unless the tool and use case are explicitly approved for that data type.
  • Verify before you rely: Treat AI output as a draft. Fact‑check content, validate citations, and confirm accuracy—especially for student‑facing guidance or policy‑related materials.
  • Be transparent: When AI meaningfully assists with a deliverable, use appropriate disclosure consistent with course expectations, departmental standards, or project requirements.
  • Mind bias and fairness: Review AI outputs for stereotypes, missing perspectives, or unfair recommendations; avoid using AI as the sole basis for consequential decisions.
  • Keep humans accountable: AI can assist with work, yet responsibility for decisions, approvals, grades, and official communications remains with CBU employees.
  • Respect intellectual property: Use proper attribution and follow licensing and copyright rules; do not assume AI‑generated content is automatically safe to reuse.

Practical starting point:

If you’re unsure whether a task is appropriate for AI, pause and ask:

  1. Am I sharing any sensitive data?
  2. Is this tool approved for CBU use?
  3. Can I independently verify the output?
  4. Who is accountable for the final result?

If any answer is unclear, consult the Policy, Training/Resources, or Approved Tools and IT Review guidance that follows.

Policy: Responsible Use of AI at CBU

Purpose

This policy establishes guidelines for the ethical, secure, and effective use of artificial intelligence (AI) tools and technologies at California Baptist University (CBU). It is intended to promote appropriate innovation while safeguarding sensitive information, maintaining academic integrity, and ensuring compliance with applicable legal, regulatory, accreditation, and contractual requirements.

Scope

This policy applies to all faculty, staff, and students who use AI tools for university‑related tasks, projects, research, instruction, or communications. Use of AI systems in connection with CBU activities constitutes agreement to comply with this policy and related university guidance.

Policy Statements

  • Approved Tools: Only AI tools that have been reviewed and approved by CBU Information Technology Services (ITS) may be used for university work. Requests for new tools, pilots, or evaluations must follow the established IT review process.
  • Data Protection: Confidential, regulated, or personally identifiable information must not be entered into AI tools unless explicit approval has been granted for the specific data type and use case.
  • Accuracy and Verification: AI‑generated content should be treated as a draft until fact‑checked and validated. Users are responsible for confirming the accuracy of outputs, particularly when providing student guidance or policy‑related information.
  • Transparency: Any meaningful use of AI in deliverables must be disclosed in accordance with course requirements, departmental standards, or project expectations.
  • Fairness and Bias: AI outputs must be reviewed for bias, omissions, or unfair recommendations. AI tools must not serve as the sole basis for significant decisions affecting individuals or groups.
  • Accountability: While AI systems may assist with tasks, ultimate responsibility for decisions, approvals, grades, evaluations, and official communications remains with CBU employees.
  • Intellectual Property: All use of AI‑generated content must comply with applicable licensing, copyright, and intellectual‑property requirements. Users must not assume AI outputs are automatically safe to reuse.

Governance

Oversight of AI use and evaluation is provided by the CBU AI Oversight Committee. Staged risk evaluation processes apply for new tools, as outlined in university procedures.

CBU AI Oversight Committee

The CBU AI Oversight Committee is responsible for guiding the university’s approach to artificial intelligence adoption, governance, and ethical use. The committee provides institutional oversight to ensure that AI technologies are implemented in ways that align with CBU’s mission, values, and regulatory obligations.

Committee membership includes representatives from academic leadership, Information Technology Services, faculty, and student services, ensuring broad and balanced perspectives across instructional, operational, and student‑facing contexts. Specific committee members will be determined and announced in an upcoming update.

The committee regularly reviews AI tools and use cases, monitors implementation practices, and provides recommendations to support the safe, effective, and responsible integration of AI technologies within the university environment. Committee guidance informs institutional policy updates, approved tool evaluations, training priorities, and staged risk‑review processes.

Faculty & Staff Guidance

Faculty and staff may use approved AI tools to support instructional design, administrative work, and research, provided human oversight is maintained and university policies are followed.
